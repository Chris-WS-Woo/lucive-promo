## Core Conversion Rules

- `Wafer` -> magical stone raw material used to create core components
- `Storage device` -> rune-inscribed grimoire or archive book that preserves records for a long time
- `CPU` -> rune-inscribed magic codex that drives calculation and spell execution
- `RAM` -> temporary memory medium such as floating memory sheets or volatile crystal pages
- `Motherboard` -> great magic circuit plate that connects all components
- `Bus` -> mana circuit or rune nerve path that transfers information
- `Power supply` -> mana furnace, spirit reactor, or heart-flame engine
- `GPU` -> illusion-specialized spellbook for visual and image-heavy magic
- `Cooling` -> frost talisman or cooling spirit apparatus that prevents mana overheating
- `OS` -> kingdom-standard operating scripture or foundational magic system
- `Program` -> packaged spell module
- `File` -> sealed spell page or record fragment
- `Network` -> resonance tower network or crystal communication grid

## Episode 1 Sample Opening

### Opening Scene Draft

Lira Vale stopped in the middle of the bridge because the note in her hand was written in her own handwriting, and she had no memory of writing it.

Around her, the morning procession toward the Royal Academy of Resonance flowed on without pause. White-stone towers caught the early light. Overhead, script-lamps drifted in the air and unfolded the day's lecture notices in pale gold letters. Students in academy cloaks crossed past her in pairs and clusters, laughing, arguing, reciting formulae under their breath before first bell.

Lira looked down again.

The paper was folded small enough to hide in her sleeve. Blue ink, slightly smudged at one corner. Definitely hers.

`If you forgot again, do not panic. Read the blue tabs first.`

That part was almost normal.

She did forget things. Names of minor saints from yesterday's lecture. Where she had left her gloves. Whether she had already eaten the honey roll in her satchel or only thought about eating it. Enough little pieces went missing from her days that she had learned, very carefully, not to be frightened by every gap.

The next line was the problem.

`Do not trust the version of events everyone else remembers.`

Lira stared at it long enough for the letters to lose shape.

"Lira."

She jerked and nearly dropped both the note and the notebook tucked under her arm. A boy had stopped a few steps away, one hand full of neatly bound lecture tablets, the other resting at his side as if he had been standing there for several seconds waiting for her to return to the world.

Kael Arden always looked as though the day had already been organized into correct proportions before sunrise. His uniform was immaculate. His dark gloves were inkless. Even the silver clasp at his collar sat with mathematical precision.

His eyes dropped once to the paper in her hand.

"First bell in three minutes," he said.

Lira folded the note at once and smiled on instinct, quick and bright enough to cover the skip in her breathing.

"Then it's fortunate you found me before I committed the academic crime of dramatic bridge loitering."

"You do that often."

"Dramatically?"

"Loitering."

That, from anyone else, would have sounded rude. From Kael it sounded like a clerical observation recorded for the sake of accuracy. Which was, strangely, less annoying.

Lira tucked the note into the notebook's blue-marked section. The notebook had seven colored tabs, a system she had refined after too many days of discovering that yesterday's Lira had once again assumed tomorrow's Lira would somehow understand everything without assistance.

Red for emergencies. Green for people. Yellow for classes. Blue for warnings she apparently was not supposed to ignore.

She was beginning to hate blue.

"Did I speak to you yesterday after practical theory?" she asked.

Kael's expression did not change, but his pause did.

"Yes."

"Long enough to explain why my own handwriting is threatening me before breakfast?"

"No."

That was all he said. No elaboration, no rescue, no easy lie made for comfort.

Which meant he knew something.

Before she could press him, the academy bell rang from the upper spire, a clean resonant tone that passed through the bridge stones and up into her bones. Students around them quickened their pace. The golden letters overhead dissolved and re-formed into directional arrows for first lecture halls.

Lira exhaled slowly.

"Please tell me today's practical exercise does not involve anything unstable, forbidden, ancient, or humiliating."

"It involves group resonance computation."

"So all four."

For a moment, something almost like amusement touched Kael's face.

"You should review your memory anchors before class," he said. "And keep your notes close."

Lira pressed the notebook tighter to her chest.

"That sounded far too much like advice from someone trying not to alarm me."

Kael met her eyes then, calm and unreadable.

"It was."

- Establishes Kael's voice and dynamic with her
- Brings in the academy atmosphere without exposition overload
- Plants suspicion before the classroom incident
- Ends with quiet unease rather than immediate spectacle

## Episode 1 Practical Class Sample

### Incident Scene Draft

The Seventh Computation Hall was built like an amphitheater for controlled disasters.

Tiered stone benches curved around a sunken practice floor engraved with concentric rune circles. Suspended above the center, six crystal prisms rotated in slow alignment, each one feeding light into the linked sequence that the second-year students had been ordered to maintain.

Professor Halwen called it a lesson in cooperative stability.

Lira privately thought it looked like an expensive way to discover who panicked first.

"Resonance teams of four," the professor said, pacing the outer ring with his hands folded behind his back. "You are not casting separate spells. You are maintaining one living structure. If your timing slips, the sequence destabilizes. If the sequence destabilizes, this hall is equipped to contain the blast."

He let the silence sit for half a beat.

"In theory."

Several students laughed uneasily.

Lira adjusted her gloves and glanced at the blue tab in her notebook one last time before sliding it shut. Kael stood across from her within the same circle, already prepared, posture straight, expression unreadable. Two other students completed their assigned team, both competent enough to be reassuring under ordinary circumstances.

Today did not remain ordinary for long.

At first the linked rune sequence rose cleanly.

Four streams of light passed from palm to prism, from prism to circle, from circle back through the etched channels beneath their feet. Formulae unfolded in the air in silver glyphs, layered and interlocking. Lira kept count the way she always did when she was nervous.

Three active rings.
Eight stabilizing marks.
One breath in.
One breath out.

Then someone on the adjacent team missed a transfer.

The mistake should not have affected them. It should have collapsed locally, triggered the containment lattice, and burned itself out into harmless sparks.

Instead, the failed sequence struck the next ring sideways, then the next, until the hall filled with a violent harmonic shudder. One of the crystal prisms above them lurched out of alignment. Another flashed white. The air changed pitch so suddenly that Lira felt it in her teeth.

"Hold your lines!" Professor Halwen shouted.

Too late.

Their team structure twisted in on itself. One of the students to Lira's left gasped and lost the pattern entirely. The silver glyphs above the circle multiplied, broke apart, and began reforming at a speed no classroom exercise should have reached.

Lira knew, dimly and humanly, that this was the moment to let the containment wards do their work.

Her body chose differently.

She stepped forward into the center line.

"Lira," Kael said sharply.

She barely heard him.

Something in the collapsing sequence opened before her not like a spell, but like a mechanism she had seen half-disassembled in a dream. The moving runes no longer looked ornamental. They looked positional. Functional. Load-bearing.

Wrong here.
Late there.
Redirect that stream.
Anchor the third layer before the seventh shear spreads.

Her hands moved.

The glyphs answered.

Gasps broke out from the benches above as the unstable sequence stopped scattering and folded toward her instead. One ring became three. Three became nine. Splintered formulae that should have needed a full faculty relay to untangle began reordering themselves in the air around Lira's outstretched arms.

She could feel all of it.

Every transfer.
Every delay.
Every pressure point across the linked structure.

Too much.
Far too much.

And still she held it.

For one impossible stretch of seconds, Lira became the center through which the entire hall recalculated itself.

The screaming pitch in the air softened. Light condensed. The damaged prism shuddered back into place.

Then pain drove white through the back of her skull.

Her thoughts did not blur. They vanished in clean, bright pieces.

The ninth ring.
Kael's voice.
Why she was standing in the center.
What day it was.

Something inside her seemed to empty all at once.

The final surge discharged upward in a pillar of harmless gold fire.

When Lira opened her eyes, she was on the practice floor with cold stone against one palm and half the hall staring at her.

Professor Halwen was saying something about extraordinary control.
Someone near the upper benches was already calling it genius.
Another voice said her name with frightened reverence, as if she had become a story in the space of a minute.

Lira looked from face to face.

"Why is everyone looking at me like that?"

The question was honest.

The silence after it was worse than the blast would have been.

Kael was the first to move. He crouched in front of her, not touching her yet, studying her face with a precision that made her suddenly aware of how hard she was breathing.

"What do you remember?" he asked.

Lira frowned.

"Practical class. Four-person sequence. You told me to review my memory anchors."

"After that?"

She searched.

Nothing. Not confusion, not fog, not fragments waiting to be gathered. Just absence, smooth and complete.

"I don't know why I'm on the floor," she said quietly.

Something changed in Kael's expression then. Not surprise. Confirmation.

Around them, the containment wards dimmed back to dormancy. The floating glyphs dissolved. Professor Halwen began ordering the other students out in clipped, shaken tones.

It was only when the hall doors opened that Lira noticed the girl standing there in white and gold archive robes.

She had not been present when class began. Lira was sure of it.

The newcomer descended the steps with calm, measured grace, one gloved hand resting over a sealed document case bound to her side. Her pale hair was braided with thin metallic threads, the style of someone attached to the Grand Archive rather than the general student body.

Every sound in the room seemed to lower itself to make way for her.

She stopped a few paces from Lira and took in the shattered residual runes still glowing faintly across the floor.

Then her gaze lifted to Lira's face.

Not curious.
Not sympathetic.

Recognizing.

"How interesting," she said softly.

Her voice was gentle enough to pass for kindness if not for the exactness of it.

"Tell me, Lady Vale. Has anyone ever asked whether your memory fails at random?"

Lira did not answer.

The archive girl tilted her head almost imperceptibly.

"Or only when you are forced to carry more than one person should?"

Kael rose to his feet between them.

"State your business, Serin."

So this was Serin.

The name meant nothing to Lira.
The feeling it left behind did.

As if somewhere inside an empty room of her mind, a locked mechanism had just turned once.

## Episode 1 Aftermath Sample

### Quiet Aftermath Draft

By late afternoon, the entire academy seemed to know what Lira had done.

Unfortunately, Lira did not.

The story reached her in fragments, each retelling polished by awe or anxiety. Someone from the upper benches insisted she had stabilized nine active layers at once. Another swore the damaged prism had cracked before she forced it back into alignment. A third lowered their voice and asked whether it was true she had rewritten a live sequence barehanded.

Lira answered each version with the same uncertain smile.

"If I did, I hope I looked impressive."

That usually earned a laugh. It also ended the conversation before anyone noticed the strain behind it.

By the time the sun dipped low enough to turn the academy windows copper, she had heard herself described as brilliant, reckless, gifted, touched by the old scripts, and once, in a whisper from two first-years who thought she was out of earshot, not entirely normal.

That last one stayed with her.

She sat alone on a narrow bench in the western cloister, her notebook open across her lap, the evening air cool against her face. The cloister overlooked one of the lower courts where practice lanterns were beginning to wake, one by one, under the arches. Students crossed below in small groups on their way to supper, voices blending into a soft and ordinary noise that made her feel, for reasons she could not explain, lonelier.

On the page before her she had written, in careful block letters:

`Things I am certain of.`

Underneath, she had managed only four lines.

- My name is Lira Vale.
- I was present in Seventh Computation Hall.
- Something happened that everyone else remembers more clearly than I do.
- Serin knew what question to ask before I knew what was wrong.

She tapped the pen once against the edge of the page.

Then added a fifth line.

- Kael was not surprised enough.

Lira leaned back and stared at the fading square of sky between the cloister arches.

Memory loss was one thing when it stole the small seams of a day. Misplaced gloves. Forgotten lunches. Half-remembered conversations that could be laughed away and mended with apologies.

This was different.

Today, the missing piece had shape.

It had witnesses.

People looked at her as though they had seen a door open and could not decide whether to call it miraculous or dangerous. She could feel the outline of that look even now, pressing at her skin.

There was a version of herself walking around in everyone else's mind.

Capable. Radiant. Impossible.

Lira had not met her.

Footsteps approached along the cloister stones, measured enough that she recognized them before she looked up.

Kael stopped beside the bench.

He had changed out of practical-class gloves, but otherwise looked exactly as he always did: composed, immaculate, built of straight lines and restraint.

"You missed supper," he said.

"I was busy being a public legend."

"You are doing it badly."

That pulled a brief laugh out of her before she could stop it.

"There she is," Lira murmured. "The legendary girl from the hall. I hope she is having a better evening than I am."

Kael's gaze dropped to the notebook. He read the lines without asking permission. Lira would have objected with anyone else. With him, it felt oddly pointless. He had probably already noticed half of them from across the courtyard.

"You should add one more," he said.

"Which is?"

"You knew how to stabilize the sequence before anyone instructed you."

Lira stared at him.

"That does not comfort me."

"It was not intended to."

She looked away again.

"Do you know what's wrong with me?"

Silence.

Not refusal. Consideration.

When Kael spoke, his voice was quieter than before.

"I know your memory failures are patterned. They occur after specific kinds of load."

"Load," Lira repeated. "That is a very unpleasant word to use about a person."

"I know."

"Do the professors know?"

"Not enough."

"Does Serin?"

His jaw tightened almost invisibly.

"Possibly too much."

Lira lowered her pen.

"That is somehow the worst answer available."

For the first time since sitting down, she sounded genuinely young.

Not witty. Not bright. Just tired.

Kael's expression shifted by a degree so small most people would have missed it.

"Lira," he said, and the way he used her name made her finally look at him, "whatever this is, you are not an object for them to claim."

The words landed with startling force.

For a moment she could not answer. She did not know whether he was reassuring her against some future danger or apologizing for already seeing it.

Before she could ask, a bell sounded from the archive quarter below, lower and stranger than the academy chimes. One note, then another, resonant enough to stir the air in the cloister.

Kael turned toward the sound at once.

"What does that mean?" Lira asked.

"Archive retrieval signal," he said.

"Should I be concerned?"

He was already looking back at her notebook.

"Yes."

That would have been an excellent moment, Lira thought later, to leave with him immediately.

Instead, after Kael was called away by a messenger from the faculty corridor, she stayed on the bench until dusk finished settling and the courtyard lamps below became full stars of amber light. Only then did she gather her things and head toward her room.

She found the loose page when she was changing the ribbon around the notebook.

It had been tucked deep between the blue and red sections where she was certain it had not been that morning.

Lira stopped beside the small desk under her dormitory window and unfolded it carefully.

At first she did not understand what she was seeing.

The page held a figure drawn in dark ink: a human body in simple lines, front-facing, almost anatomical except for the rune pathways layered through it in precise branching patterns. Notes crowded the margins in her own handwriting, but not in the easy hurried style she used during lectures. This writing was narrower. Denser. As if every stroke had been measured before it touched the page.

Across the chest, where the heart should have been, several concentric seals had been sketched and crossed through, each replaced with a cleaner technical notation.

Lira read the final label once.

Then again.

`temporary memory core`

The room did not change.

The lamp flame stayed steady. Voices still drifted faintly from the corridor beyond the door. Somewhere in the court below, someone laughed.

And yet the world had split with no sound at all.

Because under the diagram, in the same narrow hand, there was one final line.

`If you are reading this, it means the load exceeded prediction again. Do not let the Archive take you before you know what you are.`

Lira lowered herself into the chair without remembering the movement.

For a long time she did nothing but stare at the page.

Then, slowly, she turned to the first clean sheet in the notebook and began to write.

`Question 1: What am I?`
